# Behavioral Science Foundations

Ground every onboarding recommendation in at least one of these principles. Citing the principle when explaining your reasoning helps users understand *why* a tactic works, not just what to do — which lets them adapt it to their context.

## Principles

### Endowed Progress Effect
Users who feel they've already started are roughly 2x more likely to finish.

**Apply:** Pre-check the first checklist item. Start the progress bar at 20%. Phrase step 1 as "Confirm your account" (done implicitly at signup) rather than "Create your account" (perceived work).

### Zeigarnik Effect
Incomplete tasks create psychological tension that motivates action.

**Apply:** Always show "3 of 5 done" — the unfinished state pulls users back. Resurfacing reminders work because the open loop hasn't closed. Closed-loop UIs (hidden checklists) lose this pressure.

### Cognitive Load Theory
Every additional choice on a screen halves completion rate.

**Apply:** One decision per screen. Defer non-essential config to post-activation. If a screen has 4+ decisions, split it or cut them.

### Peak-End Rule
Users remember the emotional peak and the final moment — not the average experience.

**Apply:** Engineer the Aha Moment to be the peak (specific data, small celebration). Make the completion screen the end — both must feel rewarding. A boring completion screen undoes earlier excitement.

### Self-Determination Theory
Intrinsic motivation has three drivers: autonomy, competence, relatedness.

**Apply:**
- Autonomy: "Choose your path" segmentation
- Competence: "You just did X" specific feedback
- Relatedness: "Join 2,400 teams using this" social proof

### Fogg Behavior Model
Behavior = Motivation × Ability × Trigger.

**Apply:** When users drop off, diagnose which factor is missing — don't just add more steps. Low motivation → lead with outcome. Low ability → reduce steps or add templates. No trigger → add a re-engagement email or in-app nudge.

### Variable Ratio Reinforcement
Unpredictable rewards sustain engagement better than predictable ones.

**Apply:** Surprise micro-rewards (confetti, unlocked badges, "You're in the top 10% of fast starters") across multi-step flows. Don't celebrate every step identically — vary the reinforcement.

### Goal Gradient Effect
Users accelerate as they approach completion.

**Apply:** Make progress visible. Make the finish line clear. "1 more step" is more motivating than "step 4 of 5" — same information, different framing.

### Fresh Start Effect
Signup is a psychological fresh start. The first 24 hours have roughly 10x the engagement of day 7.

**Apply:** Front-load high-value actions. Don't save features for later emails if you can deliver them now. The motivation window closes fast.

### Loss Aversion
"You'll lose X" is roughly 2x more motivating than "You'll gain X."

**Apply:** Trial expiration copy ("You'll lose access to Y"), downgrade flows, incomplete-setup warnings. Use ethically — avoid manufactured loss for things users never had.

## How to Cite in Recommendations

Instead of: "Pre-check the first checklist item."

Write: "Pre-check the first checklist item — the Endowed Progress Effect means users who feel they've already started are 2x more likely to finish."

The principle name + evidence earns trust and helps users adapt the tactic to their context.
